Sec.12.4 - The Geometry of a Tetrahedron

林維德, Te-Sheng Lin (林得勝)
Last updated on May 30, 2020 2 min read Calculus

Discovery Project in Sec.12.4, Calculus by Stewart
Chinese version 四面體的幾何學

A tetrahedron is a solid with four vertices, P ,Q , R and S ,and four triangular faces, as shown in the figure.

Question 1:

Let $v_1, v_2, v_3$ and $v_4$ be vectors with lengths equal to the areas of the faces opposite the vertices P, Q, R and S, respectively, and directions perpendicular to the respective faces and pointing outward. Show that $$ v_1+v_2+v_3+v_4=0 $$

Answer:

We know that $$ v_1=\frac{\overrightarrow{QS}\times\overrightarrow{QR}}{2} , v_2=\frac{\overrightarrow{RS}\times\overrightarrow{RP}}{2} , v_3=\frac{\overrightarrow{QP}\times\overrightarrow{QS}}{2} , v_4=\frac{\overrightarrow{QR}\times\overrightarrow{QP}}{2}. $$ The vector $v_2$ can be re-written as $$ \overrightarrow{RS} = \overrightarrow{QS}-\overrightarrow{QR}, \quad \overrightarrow{RP} = \overrightarrow{QP}-\overrightarrow{QR}, \quad $$ and we have $$ \overrightarrow{RS}\times\overrightarrow{RP} = \overrightarrow{QS}\times\overrightarrow{QP}-\overrightarrow{QR}\times\overrightarrow{QP}-\overrightarrow{QS}\times\overrightarrow{QR}. $$ Therefore, by adding up the four vectors $$ v_1+v_2+v_3+v_4=\frac{\overrightarrow{QS}\times\overrightarrow{QR}+\overrightarrow{RS}\times\overrightarrow{RP}+\overrightarrow{QP}\times\overrightarrow{QS}+\overrightarrow{QR}\times\overrightarrow{QP}}{2}=0. $$

Question 2:

The volume V of a tetrahedron is one-third the distance from a vertex to the opposite face, times the area of the face.

(a)Find a formula for the volume of a tetrahedron in terms of the coordination of its vertices P ,Q , R and S.

(b)Find the volume of the tetrahedron whose vertices are P(1,1,1), Q(1,2,3), R(1,1,2), and S(3,-1,2).

Answer:

(a)

We know that the volume of a tetrahedron is one-third the distance from a vertex to the opposite face, times the area of the face. That is, it is one-sixth of the volume of a parallelepiped.

Therefore we can use the formula of the volume of the parallelepiped to form a formula for the volume of a tetrahedron.

Suppose that the three vectors that form the parallelepiped are a, b and c: $$ a=(a_1, a_2, a_3), b=(b_1, b_2, b_3), c=(c_1, c_2, c_3). $$

We now have $$ (5) \qquad V=\frac{\mid (a\times b )\cdot c \mid}{6}=\frac{\left | \begin{array}{ccc} a_1 & a_2 & a_3 \\
b_1 & b_2 & b_3 \\
c_1 & c_2 & c_3 \\
\end{array}\right |}{6} $$ In addition, suppose that P=($p_1$，$p_2$，$p_3$), Q=($q_1$，$q_2$，$q_3$), R=($r_1$，$r_2$，$r_3$), S=($s_1$，$s_2$，$s_3$).

Substitute a=Q-P, b=R-P, c=S-P into formula (5), then we can get the volume V of a tetrahedron.

(b)

By (a), a=(0,1,2), b=(0,0,1), c=(2,-2,1).

So $$ V=\frac{\left | \begin{array}{ccc} 0 & 1 & 2 \\
0 & 0 & 1 \\
2 & -2 & 1 \\
\end{array}\right |}{6}=\frac{1}{3} $$

Question 3:

Suppose the tetrahedron in the figure has a trirectangular vertex S. (This means that the three angles at S are all right angles.) Let A,B,and C be the areas of the three faces that meet at S,and let D be the area of the opposite face PQR. Using the result Question 1, or otherwise, show that $$ D^{2}=A^{2}+B^{2}+C^{2} $$ (This is a three-dimensional version of the Pythagorean Theorem.)

Answer:

First, suppose that $\overline{QS}=a$, $\overline{SR}=b$ , $\overline{SP}=c$, area PQS=A, area PSR= B, area QSR= C, and area PQR= D.

Then, we can throw the tetrahedron into a three-dimensional coordinate system, as the following figure shows.

Therefore, $$ A^{2}=\frac{a^{2}c^{2}}{4}, B^{2}=\frac{b^{2}c^{2}}{4}, C^{2}=\frac{a^{2}b^{2}}{4} $$ and we know that $$ D=\left|\frac{\overrightarrow{QP}\times\overrightarrow{QR}}{2}\right|=\left|\frac{(-a,0,c)\times(-a,b,0)}{2}\right|=\sqrt{\frac{a^2b^2+b^2c^2+a^2c^2}{4}} $$ Therefore, $$ D^2=\frac{a^2b^2+b^2c^2+a^2c^2}{4} = A^2+B^2+C^2 $$
