Sec.9.3.2 - Which is faster, going up or going down?

鄭宇皓, 簡子舜, Te-Sheng Lin (林得勝)
Last updated on May 6, 2020 2 min read Calculus

Applied Project in Sec.9.3, Calculus by Stewart
Chinese version: 上升快，還是下降快?

Suppose you throw a ball into the air. Do you think it takes longer to reach its maximum height or to fall back to earth from its maximum height? We will solve the problem in this project, but before getting started, think about that situation and make a guess based on your physical intuition.

Question 1：

A ball with mass $m$ is projected vertically upward from the earth’s surface with a positive initial velocity $v_0$. We assume the forces acting on the ball are the force of gravity and a retarding force of air resistance with direction opposite to the direction of motion and with magnitude $p|v(t)|$, where $p$ is a positive constant and $v(t)$ is the velocity of the ball at time $t$. In both the ascent and the descent, the total force acting on the ball is $-pv-mg$. [During ascent, $v(t)$ is positive and the resistance acts downward; during descent, $v(t)$ is negative and the resistance acts upward.] So, by Newton’s Second Law, the equation of motion is $$ mv'=-pv-mg $$ Solve this differential equation to show that the velocity is $$ v(t)=(v_0+\frac{mg}{p})e^{-pt/m}-\frac{mg}{p} $$

Answer:

We known that $$ mv'=-pv-mg $$ With some calculate we can get $$ \int {\frac{p}{pv+mg}dv}=\int -\frac{p}{m}dt $$ Integrate both sides we obtain $$ \ln(pv+mg) = -\frac{p}{m}t + C, \quad pv+mg=e^{-\frac{p}{m}t + C} $$ With initial condition $v(0) = v_0$ we get $$ p v_0 + mg = e^C $$ and finally we have $$ v(t)=(v_0+\frac{mg}{p})e^{-\frac{p}{m}t}-\frac{mg}{p} $$

Question 2：

Show that the height of the ball, until it hits the ground, is $$ y(t)=(v_0+\frac{mg}{p})\frac{m}{p}(1-e^{-pt/m})-\frac{mgt}{p} $$

Answer：

$$ y(t)=\int_0^tv(t)dt =\int_0^t (v_0+\frac{mg}{p})e^{-\frac{p}{m}t}-\frac{mg}{p}dt =(v_0+\frac{mg}{p})\frac{m}{p}(-e^{-\frac{p}{m}t}+1)-\frac{mg}{p}t $$

Question 3：

  1. Let $t_1$ be the time that the ball takes to reach its maximum height. Show that $$ t_1=\frac{m}{p}ln(\frac{mg+pv_0}{mg}) $$

  2. Find this time for a ball with mass 1 kg and initial velocity 20 m/s. Assume the air resistance is $\frac{1}{10}$ of the speed.

Answer：

  1. when the ball takes to reach its maximum height, the velocity $v(t_1)=0$, i.e., $$ (v_0+\frac{mg}{p})e^{-\frac{p}{m}t_1}-\frac{mg}{p}=0 $$ We then obtain $$ t_1=\frac{m}{p}\ln\left({\frac{mg + pv_0}{mg}}\right) $$

  2. with the ball with mass $m=1kg$, initial velocity $v_0=20m/s$, gravity $g=9.8m/s$, and air resistance $p=\frac{1}{10}$. $$ t_1=\frac{1}{\frac{1}{10}}ln\left({\frac{1\times9.8+\frac{1}{10}\times20}{1\times9.8}}\right)=‭1.85717‬s $$

Question 4：

Let $t_2$ be the time at which the ball falls back to earth. For the particular ball in Problem 3, estimate $t_2$ by using a graph of the height function $y(t)$. Which is faster, going up or coming down?

Answer：

because the time of the ball going up is shorter than the ball coming down, the speed of the ball going up is faster than the coming down.

Question 5：

In general, it’s not easy to find $t_2$ because it’s impossible to solve the equation $y(t)=0$ explicitly. We can, however, use an indirect method to determine whether ascent or descent is faster: we determine whether $y(2t_1)$ is positive or negative.

  1. Show that $$ y(2t_1)=\frac{m^2g}{p^2}(x-\frac{1}{x}-2\ln{x}) $$ where $x=e^{pt_1/m}$.

  2. Then show that $x > 1$ and the function $$ f(x)=x-\frac{1}{x}-2\ln{x} $$ is increasing for $x > 1$.

  3. Use this result to decide whether $y(2t_1)$ is positive or negative. What can you conclude? Is ascent or descent faster?

Answer：

  1. According to Question 3, we get $$ t_1=\frac{m}{p}ln{\frac{pv_0+mg}{mg}},　\quad x=e^{pt_1/m}, \quad \Rightarrow \quad x=\frac{pv_0+mg}{mg} $$ According to the Question 2, we get $$ y(2t_1)=(v_0+\frac{mg}{p})\frac{m}{p}(1-e^{-2pt_1/m})-\frac{2mgt_1}{p} $$ $$ =\frac{m^2g}{p^2}\left[(1-e^{-2pt_1/m})+(\frac{pv_0}{mg})(1-e^{-2pt_1/m})-(\frac{2pt_1}{m})\right] $$ $$ =\frac{m^2g}{p^2}\left[(1-\frac{1}{x^2})+(\frac{pv_0}{mg})(1-\frac{1}{x^2})-2\ln x\right] =\frac{m^2g}{p^2}(x-\frac{1}{x}-2\ln{x}) $$

  2. According to the question $$ f(x)=x-\frac{1}{x}-2\ln{x}, \quad f’(x)=1+\frac{1}{x^2}-\frac{2}{x} =(\frac{x-1}{x})^2\geq 0 $$ so we know that $f(x)$ is increasing for $x>1$

  3. By the result, we know that $f(1)=0$, and $f(x)$ is increasing for $x>1$, so we can conclude that $$ y(2t_1)=\frac{m^2g}{p^2}(x-\frac{1}{x}-2\ln{x})>0 $$ Because $y(2t_1)$ is positive, which means that the $2t_1<t_1+t_2$, and $t_1<t_2$. So the conclusion is that ascent is faster than descent.
